NEPI Atlas — About

← Back to map

The Neighbourhood Energy Performance Index (NEPI) rates every Output Area in England — small neighbourhoods of about 130 households — on the total energy cost of living there. It's an EPC-style A–G rating, but for the place, not the building.

What this is, in three sentences

One. NEPI is a place-level energy rating: every Output Area (a neighbourhood of ~130 households) gets an A–G band based on the total energy footprint of living there.

Two. The score is built from three real, observed quantities per OA — metered gas + electricity (Form), Census-derived transport energy (Mobility), and an empirical penalty for poor walkable service coverage (Access). They sum in kWh/household/year and are banded by national percentile.

Three. The dashboard lets you view this from different angles: by individual surface (Form / Mobility / Access), in different units (kWh / kgCO₂ / £), and projected forward through the official UK grid-decarbonisation pathway. The scenarios show how today's actual energy mix ages under tomorrow's electricity grid — they do not model retrofit, heat-pump, or EV adoption.

What this is NOT — important for grounding:
  • Not a forecast. "2050 mode" doesn't predict 2050 reality. It applies the projected 2050 grid-carbon factor to today's per-OA fuel data.
  • Not a model of demand changes. Heat-pump rollout, EV adoption, fabric retrofit — none of these are modelled. kWh values are held constant across scenario years.
  • Not policy. NEPI describes how energy intensity varies by place. It doesn't recommend interventions.
  • Not individual. The unit of analysis is the OA — every household in an OA gets the same NEPI score. Tells you about average outcomes for a place, not specific households.
  • Not all of England. Coverage is OAs whose centroid falls within an OS Built-Up Area polygon — i.e. urban, suburban, and village settlements. ~12,000 isolated rural OAs are out of scope (they need a different framing entirely; rural mobility patterns and the walkable-access idea don't translate). Net coverage: ~177,000 of England's ~189,000 OAs (~98% of households).

The headline idea

An EPC rates a dwelling's envelope. But an EPC misses the rest of the energy story: how much you drive because shops are far, how much heat your terrace shares with the neighbours, whether your nearest GP, school, and bus stop are walkable. NEPI puts those things on one scale, in one unit (kWh/household/year), so they can be compared and combined without arbitrary weighting.

Bands are set by national percentile of total energy. Lower kWh = better. A national flat-dominant OA averages 15,982 kWh/hh/yr (Band A); a detached-dominant OA averages 26,897 kWh/hh/yr (Band F) — a 1.7× spread.

Three surfaces

The composite NEPI is the sum of three independent surfaces. Each captures a distinct mechanism by which form drives energy use. Form and Mobility are direct measurements / fuel-intensity estimates; Access is a basket index — a derived, empirical quantity, not a metered value.

At a typical OA: Form ≈ 60% of the total, Mobility ≈ 35%, Access (basket) ≈ 5%. At a sprawling detached suburb: Form 65%, Mobility 30%, Access (basket) 6%. Note that the Access figure is a conservative basket-index estimate — the real scale of the access-driven energy gap is larger than the basket captures (residents in sprawling places make more trips and longer ones than the basket assumes, while compact-area residents forgo trips entirely).

Form — building energy

Metered gas + electricity per household, gas weather-corrected to heat-degree-day standard. Compact dwelling types (flats, terraces) lose less heat per unit floor area than detached houses because of lower surface-to-volume ratios and shared party walls.

National range 6,900 – 13,500 median – 21,300 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95)
Source: DESNZ postcode-level domestic energy consumption, aggregated to OA via postcode→OA spatial lookup. Excludes non-domestic. Direct measurement.

Mobility — transport energy

Estimated household transport energy from Census 2021 commute distance × travel mode × fuel intensity (ECUK). Captures the bulk of routine travel; not a perfect proxy for total transport energy but the strongest available open data source.

National range 2,400 – 7,400 median – 14,300 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95)
Source: ONS Census 2021 (TS058 distance, TS061 mode), DESNZ ECUK fuel intensities by mode. Behaviour-derived estimate using observed commute behaviour and per-mode fuel-intensity factors.

Access — coverage penalty Indicative basket index

Important: this is not a directly measured energy value. Access is an indicative basket index — a synthetic per-OA quantity approximating the extra transport energy attributable to poor walkable service coverage. The index assumes a typical mix of trips for typical land-use categories (food, healthcare, education, transit, greenspace, retail) and combines them via Gaussian-decay walking thresholds across nine service types (shops, takeaways, pubs, GPs, pharmacies, schools, greenspace, bus stops, hospitals).

The penalty is calibrated by an OLS regression of observed commute-transport energy on coverage, controlling for density, household size, deprivation, and build year. For each OA we predict its kWh penalty relative to a compact reference (85th-percentile coverage — the median of flat-dominant OAs). Compact OAs incur ~0 penalty; sprawling OAs accumulate up to ~2,500 kWh/hh/yr in the index.

The real scale of difference is greater. The basket models typical-trips × typical land uses and is calibrated against observed commute energy. Real-world behaviour amplifies the gap further: residents in sprawling places generate more trips and longer ones than the basket assumes, while residents in walkable places forgo some trips entirely or chain them efficiently. The Access index here is a conservative lower bound on the actual accessibility-driven energy gap between compact and sprawling OAs — not an upper bound.
National range 0 – 630 median – 2,500 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95). Half of all OAs incur 0–630.
Source: cityseer network analysis on OS Open Roads + FSA, NaPTAN, GIAS, NHS ODS, OS Greenspace destinations. Penalty model: OLS with HC1 robust standard errors. Indicative basket index, not metered energy. Conservative — real differences are larger.

What the dashboard shows you

Surface Composite shows the A–G band of the total NEPI. Each individual surface shows just that pillar on a continuous green→red ramp anchored at the national p5–p95 of that surface.
Unit kWh, kgCO₂e, or £/year. Per-surface conversion factors: Form blends gas (70%) + electricity (30%); Mobility blends petrol + diesel; Access uses Mobility's factor (the Access surface is a transport-energy penalty). Bands stay constant across units — switching the unit rescales display values and legend labels without re-banding.

Click any OA on the map to see its band, the breakdown across the three surfaces, its archetype (Flat/Terraced/Semi/Detached dominant), and how it compares to (1) the national median and (2) other OAs of the same archetype.

Two OAs in the same band can have very different mixes. An A-band city centre flat might have low Form (small dwelling, shared walls) and low Mobility (short commute) with zero Access penalty. An A-band rural village could be A-band only because it's a tight cluster — high Form, low Mobility, near-zero Access — different policy levers entirely. The surface decomposition is the point.

Methodological caveats

Ecological inference. The unit of analysis is the OA. NEPI tells you about average outcomes for a place, not specific households (Robinson 1950 ecological-fallacy caveat). That's the right level for the question — neighbourhood form is a property of the place, not the resident — but worth stating explicitly.

Metered where possible, modelled where not. EPCs systematically over-predict consumption (the "performance gap"); we sidestep this by using DESNZ postcode-level metered data for the Form surface. Mobility and Access are still derived from Census behaviour because nobody meters travel directly at this granularity.

Per-OA fuel mix used for Form scenarios; national blends for Mobility. Form CO₂ and £ values use each OA's actual gas/electricity split (from DESNZ postcode data), so OAs experience grid decarbonisation differently. Mobility and Access still use a national petrol/diesel blend because per-OA petrol/diesel data is not available — a real limitation, called out honestly.

Limitations

  • Census 2021 commute data was COVID-affected. Robustness check: the pre-pandemic Census 2011 transport gradient is steeper than 2021 (flat-vs-detached: 2.00× in 2011 vs 1.70× in 2021). The dashboard uses 2021 data, so the morphology-energy gradient you see here is a conservative lower bound. Full robustness section in PAPER.md §5.
  • Spatial autocorrelation is partial. BUA-clustered standard errors are used, but spatial error / lag models are forward work.
  • Mobility doesn't capture leisure / shopping / school-run travel. Only commute-to-work behaviour is observed; the Access penalty fills part of this gap empirically but it's a proxy, not a measurement.
  • 442 BUAs missing from the combined dataset. A pre-2026-05-07 path-collision bug in the upstream pipeline overwrote one BUA's data with another's whenever two shared a sanitised name. These BUAs are correctly absent rather than incorrectly present and will be reinstated by a targeted reprocess. Greater Manchester (this Atlas) is unaffected — Manchester's BUA22CD is unique.
  • Future-scenario factors are central trajectories. NESO FES Holistic Transition + DESNZ Fossil Fuel Price Assumptions central case. Low/high envelopes exist in the input data ([data/projections/](https://github.com/UCL/urban-energy/tree/main/data/projections)) but aren't yet exposed in the UI.

Status & roadmap

Live:

  • B1 — CO₂ + £ unit toggles. ✅ DUKES emission factors and Ofgem-cap-based £/kWh applied as per-surface multipliers on the existing kWh fields. Bands stay invariant across units.
  • B3 — Projection data pipeline. ✅ Year-keyed factor tables loaded from CSV with per-row source citation (NESO FES Holistic Transition for grid carbon, DESNZ Fossil Fuel Price Assumptions for prices). See data/projections/.
  • B4 — Scenario mode. ✅ 2030 / 2040 / 2050 toggles active. Selected-OA panel adds a "vs same OA in 2025" row when in scenario mode. Energy demand (kWh) is held constant; only the unit-conversion factors evolve.

Open:

  • National scope. Currently covers Greater Manchester (8,886 OAs). Extending to all 177,050 OAs nationally is a tippecanoe-tuning exercise, not a methodology change.
  • Demand-side electrification. Heat-pump and EV rollouts would change kWh per OA, not just kgCO₂ per kWh. Out of scope for v0; would be a per-OA scenario-year demand multiplier on top of the existing factor structure.
  • Sensitivity envelopes. The price CSV carries low/central/high scenarios; the dashboard exposes only central. Adding low/high toggles is a small UI extension.
  • 442 BUAs needing reprocess. A pre-fix path-collision bug left these with overwritten data; targeted re-run of processing/pipeline_oa.py for affected BUA22CDs will fill the gap.
