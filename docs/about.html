NEPI Atlas — About

← Back to map

The Neighbourhood Energy Performance Index (NEPI) rates every Output Area in England — small neighbourhoods of about 130 households — on the total energy cost of living there. It's an EPC-style A–G rating, but for the place, not the building.

What this is

NEPI is a place-level energy rating. Every Output Area in England — a neighbourhood of approximately 130 households — receives an A–G band derived from the total domestic energy footprint of living there.

The composite score combines three observed quantities per OA: metered gas and electricity (Form), Census-derived transport energy (Mobility), and an empirical penalty for poor walkable service coverage (Access). Values are summed in kWh per household per year and banded by national percentile.

The dashboard exposes this through three controls: a Surface selector (composite or one of the three pillars), a Unit selector (kWh, kgCO₂, £), and a forward-projection block that applies the official UK grid-decarbonisation trajectory together with hypothetical heat-pump and EV adoption levels. The Lock-in surface shows the residual demand under full electrification — energy attributable to urban form alone.

What this is not
  • Not a forecast. A future-year scenario applies projected grid-carbon factors to today's per-OA fuel data; it does not predict the actual state of any future year.
  • Not policy. NEPI describes how energy intensity varies by place. It does not recommend interventions.
  • Not individual. The unit of analysis is the OA. Every household in an OA receives the same NEPI score. Results describe average outcomes for a place, not specific households.
  • Not all of England. Coverage extends to all 7,147 OS Built-Up Areas — approximately 177,050 of England's 189,000 OAs (~98 % of households). The remaining ~12,000 isolated rural OAs are out of scope: rural mobility patterns and the walkable-access framing do not translate to settlements with no built-up footprint.

Concept

An EPC rates a dwelling envelope. NEPI rates the place. The envelope is one determinant of energy demand among several: heat lost through walls and roofs, transport energy required by daily travel, and the additional energy demand attributable to poor walkable service coverage. NEPI combines these on a single scale (kWh per household per year) so they can be compared and aggregated without arbitrary weighting.

Bands are set by national percentile of total energy. Lower kWh = better. A national flat-dominant OA averages 15,982 kWh/hh/yr (Band A); a detached-dominant OA averages 26,897 kWh/hh/yr (Band F) — a 1.7× spread.

The pillars

The composite NEPI is the sum of three pillars, each capturing a distinct mechanism by which urban form influences household energy demand. Form is a metered measurement of gas and electricity. Mobility is a behaviour-derived estimate from Census travel data and per-mode fuel intensities. The Access index is a derived empirical quantity calibrated against observed transport energy.

At a typical OA, Form contributes ~60 % of the composite, Mobility ~35 %, the Access index ~5 %. At a sprawling detached suburb, the split is ~65 / 30 / 6. The Access index uses conservative minimum assumptions; the true accessibility-driven energy gap is larger.

Form — building energy

Metered gas + electricity per household, gas weather-corrected to heat-degree-day standard. Compact dwelling types (flats, terraces) lose less heat per unit floor area than detached houses because of lower surface-to-volume ratios and shared party walls.

National range 6,900 – 13,500 median – 21,300 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95)
Source: DESNZ postcode-level domestic energy consumption, aggregated to OA via postcode→OA spatial lookup. Excludes non-domestic. Direct measurement.

Mobility — transport energy

Estimated household transport energy from Census 2021 commute distance × travel mode × fuel intensity (ECUK). The Census records only commute-to-work travel, which understates true mobility demand: leisure, shopping, school-run, and other discretionary trips are not represented. Mobility values published here are therefore a lower bound on the per-OA total transport energy. Census 2021 is also the most recent open dataset at OA granularity; subsequent travel pattern shifts are not captured.

National range 2,400 – 7,400 median – 14,300 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95)
Source: ONS Census 2021 (TS058 distance, TS061 mode), DESNZ ECUK fuel intensities by mode. Behaviour-derived estimate using observed commute behaviour and per-mode fuel-intensity factors.

Access index — coverage penalty

The Access index is a derived empirical quantity, not a metered energy value. It approximates the additional transport energy per household attributable to poor walkable service coverage. Construction proceeds in two steps. First, a basket of typical trips to typical land uses (food retail, schools, GPs, pharmacies, transit stops, greenspace, hospitals, pubs, takeaways) is evaluated against each OA's network distance to those services, weighted by Gaussian decay around service-specific walking thresholds. Second, the resulting coverage score is mapped to a kWh penalty by OLS regression on observed commute-transport energy, controlling for density, household size, deprivation, and build year. The penalty is expressed relative to a compact reference (85th-percentile coverage; median of flat-dominant OAs).

The index uses conservative minimum assumptions: the basket models a fixed set of typical trips, calibration is against commute-only energy, and the resulting estimates are clipped at zero. Real behavioural differences are larger. Residents of sprawling places generate more, and longer, trips than the basket assumes; residents of walkable places routinely forgo trips or chain them efficiently. The published Access values are therefore a lower bound on the true accessibility-driven energy gap. The pillar is retained in the composite — and made prominent in the dashboard — because the structural mechanism it captures is robust even where the calibrated magnitude is conservative.

National range 0 – 630 median – 2,500 kWh/hh/yr (p5 – p50 – p95). Approximately half of all OAs incur a penalty between 0 and 630 kWh/hh/yr.
Source: cityseer network analysis on OS Open Roads, with destination layers from FSA, NaPTAN, GIAS, NHS ODS, OS Open Greenspace. Penalty model: OLS with HC1 robust standard errors. Conservative basket index; not metered energy.

Dashboard controls

Control Function
Surface Selects the variable mapped: Composite (A–G bands on the total NEPI), one of the three pillars (Form, Mobility, Access index), or Lock-in (residual under full electrification). Pillar surfaces use a continuous ramp anchored at the national p5–p95 of that pillar.
Unit Applies a per-surface conversion factor to express values in kWh, kgCO₂, or £ per household per year. Form blends gas (~70 %) and electricity (~30 %); Mobility blends petrol and diesel; the Access index inherits Mobility's transport factor. Bands are invariant across units; switching the unit rescales values and legend labels without re-banding.
Forward projection A future grid year and hypothetical adoption levels for heat pumps and electric vehicles. The grid year applies the projected DESNZ / NESO factors to today's per-OA fuel data. The Adoption sliders scale demand: heat-pump adoption shifts a proportional share of gas demand to electricity at COP 3; EV adoption shifts petrol and diesel demand to electricity at a 3.5× efficiency ratio. The projection block is disabled when Surface = Lock-in (which fixes its own scenario) or Unit = kWh (kWh values are not grid-projected).

Selecting an OA reveals its band, its breakdown across the three pillars, its dominant accommodation archetype (Flat, Terraced, Semi, or Detached), and comparisons against the national median and the median of OAs sharing its archetype.

Two OAs in the same band may differ substantially in surface composition. A city-centre flat reaches Band A through low Form (shared walls, small floor area) combined with low Mobility (short commute) and a near-zero Access penalty. A compact rural village can reach Band A through tight clustering: relatively high Form, low Mobility, and a small Access penalty. The two profiles imply different intervention levers; the per-pillar decomposition exposes this.

Methodological caveats

Ecological inference. The unit of analysis is the OA. NEPI describes average outcomes for a place, not specific households (Robinson 1950 ecological-fallacy caveat).

Form is metered; Mobility and Access are derived. Form uses DESNZ postcode-level metered domestic energy aggregated to OA. EPC predictions are not used (the well-documented performance gap between predicted and metered EPC consumption is therefore avoided). Mobility and Access are derived from Census 2021 commute behaviour combined with per-mode fuel-intensity factors; metered transport energy is not available at OA granularity.

Per-OA fuel split for Form; national blend for Mobility and Access. Form CO₂ and £ values use each OA's actual gas / electricity split from DESNZ postcode data. Mobility and Access use a national petrol / diesel blend because per-OA petrol / diesel data is not available at this granularity.

Limitations

  • Census 2021 commute data was COVID-affected. The pre-pandemic Census 2011 transport gradient (flat-vs-detached: 2.00×) is steeper than the 2021 gradient (1.70×). The dashboard uses 2021 data; the published morphology-energy gradient is therefore a conservative lower bound. Full robustness analysis: PAPER.md §5.
  • Spatial autocorrelation. Standard errors are BUA-clustered. Spatial error and spatial lag models are not applied.
  • Mobility scope. Census records commute-to-work travel only; leisure, shopping, school-run, and other discretionary trips are not represented. The Access index addresses this gap empirically but is a derived proxy, not a direct measurement.
  • Future-scenario factors are central trajectories. NESO FES Holistic Transition for grid carbon; DESNZ Fossil Fuel Price Assumptions central case for prices. Low and high envelopes are present in the input data but are not currently exposed in the dashboard.
